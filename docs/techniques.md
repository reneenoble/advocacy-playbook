# Advocacy Techniques

This page covers the core techniques advocacy teams use to promote products, samples, and tools.

## 1. Storytelling

The most effective advocacy starts with a compelling story. Before creating any piece of content, ask:

- **Who** is the developer audience?
- **What** problem does this product or tool solve for them?
- **Why** should they care right now?
- **How** does this change the way they work?

### The Developer Journey Arc

A strong advocacy narrative follows the developer's journey:

1. **Problem** – State the pain point your audience relates to.
2. **Discovery** – Introduce the product/tool as a solution.
3. **Exploration** – Walk through how it works.
4. **Success** – Show the outcome: what the developer can now do.
5. **Call to action** – Give the audience a clear next step (try it, star the repo, read the docs).

## 2. Live Demos

Live demos are one of the most powerful advocacy tools — they show, rather than tell.

### Demo Best Practices

- **Start with the end** – Show the finished result first, then walk back to show how to build it. This builds anticipation.
- **Keep it focused** – A 5-minute focused demo is more effective than a 20-minute comprehensive one.
- **Have a backup plan** – Keep a recorded version or screenshots ready in case of live demo failures.
- **Use real scenarios** – Demos that solve a recognizable problem land better than contrived examples.
- **Practice out loud** – Rehearse your demo narration, not just the keystrokes.

### Demo Types

| Demo Type | When to Use |
|---|---|
| Live coding | Conference talks, workshops, office hours |
| Recorded screencast | YouTube, social media, documentation |
| Interactive notebook (e.g., Codespaces, Jupyter) | Tutorials, workshops, hackathons |
| Slide-based with screenshots | Introductory awareness content |

## 3. Samples and Code Examples

Samples are a key deliverable for advocacy teams. A great sample should:

- **Solve a real problem** that developers actually face.
- **Be minimal** — include only what is needed to demonstrate the concept.
- **Be runnable** — developers should be able to clone and run it in under 5 minutes.
- **Be well documented** — include a README that explains what it does, how to run it, and what to learn from it.

See [Samples & Demos](samples-and-demos.md) for a full guide on creating high-quality samples.

## 4. Written Tutorials and Blog Posts

### Tutorial Best Practices

- Use short, clear headings to make the content scannable.
- Include code blocks with syntax highlighting for all code snippets.
- Add screenshots or diagrams where helpful.
- Always include a "what you'll learn" section at the start.
- End with a "next steps" section pointing to further resources.

### Blog Post Formats

| Format | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| How-to | Step-by-step instructions | Tutorials, quick-starts |
| Explainer | Explains a concept or technology | New features, complex topics |
| Story / case study | Narrative around a real use case | Community interest, customer stories |
| Listicle | "Top 5 tips for…" | Tips, resources, roundups |
| Opinion / perspective | First-person point of view | Thought leadership, community engagement |

## 5. Video and Streaming

Video content is highly engaging and shareable, and reaches audiences who prefer watching to reading.

### Short-form Video (< 2 minutes)

- Great for social media (LinkedIn, X/Twitter, Instagram, TikTok).
- Focus on a single tip, feature, or wow moment.
- Add captions — most people watch without sound.

### Long-form Video (5–20 minutes)

- YouTube, conference recordings, webinars.
- Best for walkthroughs, demos, and tutorials.
- Add chapters to make the content navigable.

### Live Streaming

- Twitch, YouTube Live, LinkedIn Live.
- Great for community engagement and authenticity.
- Works well for "build with me" sessions and Q&A.

## 6. Talks and Presentations

Conference talks are a high-effort, high-impact advocacy channel.

### Talk Structure

1. **Hook** – Open with a memorable question, stat, or story.
2. **Context** – Set the scene and frame the problem.
3. **Demo / Content** – The core of the talk.
4. **Key takeaways** – Summarise the 3 things you want the audience to remember.
5. **Call to action** – Tell the audience exactly what to do next.

### Tips for Preparing Talks

- Start crafting your talk abstract before developing the full content.
- Rehearse with a friendly audience before the actual event.
- Share slides and code samples publicly after the event.
- Promote your talk on social media before and after.

## 7. Workshops and Hands-on Labs

Workshops provide an immersive learning experience and are ideal for:

- Conferences and meetups
- Internal Microsoft/GitHub developer events
- Hackathons

### Workshop Design Principles

- Each module should be completable in 20–30 minutes.
- Provide a working starting point so participants don't spend time on setup.
- Include clear checkpoints so participants can verify they are on track.
- Use GitHub Codespaces or similar to minimize environment setup friction.
- Always test the full workshop flow end-to-end before running it.

---

## 8. Community Engagement

Advocacy is most effective when it's a two-way conversation.

### Engagement Tactics

- **Reply and respond** – Actively engage with comments on your content.
- **Answer questions** – Monitor Stack Overflow, GitHub Discussions, and developer forums for questions related to your product.
- **Acknowledge contributions** – Thank community members who build on your samples or share your content.
- **Host office hours / AMAs** – Offer regular opportunities for developers to ask questions directly.
- **Amplify community content** – Share and celebrate content created by the community about your product.

---

## 9. Internal Feedback Loop

Advocacy is not just outbound — it's also about bringing developer feedback back into the organization.

- Maintain a regular cadence of sharing community feedback with the product team.
- Highlight friction points developers encounter in samples, docs, or the product itself.
- Advocate internally for developer experience improvements based on what you hear in the community.
